Reality Photo Guidance
How to put a photo in a poster

Every photo
gets printed.

A raw photo never goes straight onto a REALITY poster — it runs through one ink treatment so it prints like the rest of the system. This page is the short version: which treatment for which job, which paper for which night, and how the logo sits on top. The live tool is the Poster Studio; this is the rulebook.

01

Pick a treatment

Start with Duotone. Reach for the others when the photo or the night needs more punch.
Duotone

Two-Colour Sep · the default

Keeps the whole photo legible in two inks. If you're unsure, use this — it never fights the layout.

Best for

Faces, portraits, anything that has to stay readable.

Avoid when

You want maximum graphic punch — go louder below.

Off-Register

Off-Register · the signature

Two ink passes shifted apart — the misprint. Carries the most REALITY energy.

Best for

Hero DJ / party shots, movement, big sizes.

Avoid when

The photo is text-dense or printed small — fringing muddies it.

Halftone

Halftone · newsprint grit

One ink as a dot screen. Cheap-print, gig-poster feel. Loves contrast.

Best for

Live music, crowds, high-contrast images.

Avoid when

The photo is soft and flat — the dots vanish.

Banded

Banded · silkscreen flatness

Tones crushed to a few flat steps on palette inks. Posterised, not photographic.

Best for

Bold, simple compositions with one clear subject.

Avoid when

The scene is detailed — fine detail collapses.

Cutout

Ink Cutout · max punch

Lit subject knocked out of a solid accent field. Reads from across a dark room.

Best for

One clear subject on a dark background; loud headlines.

Avoid when

The background is busy — the subject won't separate.

Overprint

Overprint · wet overlap

Two flat inks overlap and bloom a third colour. The richest, most abstract look.

Best for

Texture / abstract shots, covers, two-colour richness.

Avoid when

Literal clarity matters — it abstracts the image.

02

Day or night paper

Paper sets the whole mood. Match it to the event, then pick an accent from that side.
Day paperCream · #FFFBF1
Use forDaytime, community, sundowner, markets, workshops, all-ages.
AccentsBlue · Amber · Green — the open, friendly end.
TreatmentsDuotone & Banded sit warmest and most legible on cream.
MoodEditorial, approachable, easy to read in daylight.
Night paperNear-black · #0A0703
Use forClub nights, DJ sets, live music, late parties.
AccentsPink · Red · Purple — the loud, after-dark end.
TreatmentsOff-Register, Halftone, Cutout & Overprint hit hardest on black.
MoodHigh-energy, electric, glows in a dark room.
03

The Reality logo box

The wordmark never floats bare on a treated photo — it always sits in a box.

Contain it

The REALITY wordmark always rides a solid paper or ink box — most often the ticket footer. Never set it bare over a busy treated photo.

Clear space

Keep a margin equal to the wordmark's cap-height on all four sides of the box. Nothing else enters that zone.

Contrast first

If the photo behind is loud, the box stays opaque. The treatment runs behind the lockup, never under the letters themselves.

04

Quick rules

Do

  • Start from a high-contrast source — treatments live on tonal range.
  • Let one ink lead; follow the poster accent unless there's a reason not to.
  • Size the halftone dots to the room — bigger for a poster, finer for a phone story.
  • Export at 1080px wide minimum; use the A4 format for anything printed.

Don't

  • Drop a full-colour photo in raw — it breaks the locked palette.
  • Stack treatments or invent off-palette tints.
  • Run the wordmark over a bare treated photo — use the box.
  • Use Cutout on a busy background, or tiny Halftone on a small format.
